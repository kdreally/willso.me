Definite article declension

Case Masculine Neuter Feminine Plural
Nominative þe þat se þo
Accusative þone þat þo þo
Dative þun þun þer þon
Genitive þes þes þer þor
Instrumental þon þon þer þon

This is based on a mixture of the OE article system and native palatal developments in Middle English. I originally thought þet should replace þat (or þæt), but the reason the pronunciation shifted could be due to the ongoing French influence on grammar at the time.


Indefinite Article declension

A and An are used today exclusively as the indefinite article, coming from Old English an. What was forgotten in Varad's Reactionary Grammar section (Sticky II of the Ewbook 2.0) is that "an" is in fact New English "one". Perhaps due to Norman influence, the formation of an → one in early ME became mixed with French on and as a result the older and newer form of the word split. This is further supported by how an was used in Old English as well as fellow West Germanic languages today (Dutch een and German ein) where "one" is the indefinite article and also "a particular" (i.e OE an of ðam → one of those). In a way, this means that our current understanding that "one" meaning "a particular" being from French influence is misconstrued. What's more likely is the underdevelopment of a/an is a result of French influence instead.

Case Masc. Neuter Fem.
Nominative one one one
Accusative onen onen onen
Dative one one oner
Genitive ones ones oner
Instrumental one one oner

This chart is based on the one above -- a mixture of the pre-Norman declension and how the pronunciation shifts by New English (based on comparisons).


Demonstratives

The last declension I will mention is the demonstratives. This is because the others (such as pronouns, adjectives and numbers) were already being lost in late Old English. For me, these few rules feel like English when put into action. It feels like assembling lost pieces to complete an unfinished puzzle.

This Masc. Neuter Fem. Plural
Nominative þese þis þese þose
Accusative þissen þis þese þose
Dative þissen þissen þisser þissen
Genitive þisses þisses þisser þeser
Instrumental þise þise þisser þissen

As for That, one could assume it follows similar declension to the definitive article since it was the original neuter singular it. This is an assumption, so take it with a grain of salt, but it should work for Willsome English.

That Singular Plural
Nominative þat þose
Accusative þatten þosen
Genitive þats þoser

Examples

Now some example sentences with the above grammar in WSE:

  • He spurn onen stone. (He kicked a stone)
  • Se girl and oner freend gede to þæt learninghuse. (The girl and a friend of hers went to the school)
  • Ic slayed þe/þæt shuck wiþ þon holy mece. (I slayed the demon with the holy sword)
  • He fanged þæt book þes Hunter. (He took Hunter's book.)
  • Þisser lady was mine wiffe. (This lady was my wife.)
  • He struck þæt ore wiþ þise bill. (He struck the/that ore with this pickaxe)
