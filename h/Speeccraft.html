First, I will address the rules for inflection in Willsome English based on those in Old English, modernized lightly. There are 5 major cases: The Namenlic (Nominative), the Wragenlic (Accusative), the Forgifenlic (Dative), the Astreenenlic (Genitive), and the Toolfyllenlic (Instrumental). This is more conservative than even Icelandic, which fails to retain the Instrumental.

There were three genders in old English, which are often called masculine, feminine, and neuter, but the way English handled these was rather complicated. It would naturally simplify a little into a more basic rule: The feminine is used for specifically feminine things, like women or a female animal (as well as concepts like learning, need, rest, and charity) whereas everything animate that is not explicitly female is referred to in the masculine & everything inanimate (but for concepts, which are treated as feminine) is treated with a neuter gender.

There were three numbers in Old English, but the dual form was only used in a set of first and second person pronouns. Otherwise, only singular and plural forms exist (dual pronouns are treated as plurals).

There are three moods in Old, and by extension, Willsome English. The Sayenlic mete (Indicative mood) is used when the speaker is making a statement, the Wishenlic (Subjunctive) is used when the speaker is expressing a wish, reporting something, making a statement contrary to facts, or talking about something conditional on other variables. The Biddenlic (Imperative) is used only when the speaker is making a command.

There are 3 major tenses (tides) in Willsome English: Forþawitten (Past), Anward (Present), & Toward (Future). Each of these has Strait (Simple), Ongoing (Continuous), and Wholemade (Perfect) shapes.

Names


Old English had big boy noun declension. First, every noun had a gender. Animate things were male, unless you knew or wanted to emphasize that they were female or feminine, in which case they were feminine. Concepts (like help, rest, sleep, or thrill) are also feminine. Everything else, from rocks to twigs to the Sun, is neuter. Second, every noun had a final sound. Like horn, which ended with a consonant, or oxa (ox) which ended with a vowel. There were words that ended with -u in Orþeec (the earliest Germanic language) which had their own declension. Others that ended in -u or -o fell into a different class. These types were called strong, weak, u, and ƿ "a-stems", respectively. There also exist so called I-mutants and indeclinables (like lengu, which does not refer to the South Indian attire, but instead to the word for length, which, being a concept, was feminine.). What an a-stem is, nobody knows (and everybody who tells you otherwise is lying to you).

Adjectives


Adjectives are word that add information, hence they are called ekers (adders) in Willsome English. The adjective is declined to agree with the noun in case, gender, and number. Strong adjectives are inflected for all three separately. Since I cannot make 3D tables, I will use a single table for all cases, all three genders, and both numbers. Any adjective can be used weakly or strongly. Weakly when it was used as part of a nickname, or when it followed a demonstrative, an article, or a posessive pronoun of the first or second person. Strongly in all other cases. Note that some adjectives in OE were universally weak, like most ordinal numbers, and others, like oðer (second) were universally strong. Note also that all comparatives (better, worse, longer, etc.) have weak declension. Weak adjectival declension was the same as weak nounal declension, for which see above or click here.

Adjectives ending in o or u, much like with nouns, have that turn into a w when the grammatical suffixes they are inflected with start with a vowel. Monosyllabic adjectives ending with æ followed by a consonant (glad, sad, bad, etc.) had it turn into an "a" when grammatical suffixes start with a vowel, back in Old English. There's no reason that couldn't disappear as time went on, but there's no reason it should, either.
